Everything in the universe ‚Äî from the air we breathe to the stars in the sky ‚Äî is made up of atoms. An atom is the smallest unit of matter that still has the properties of an element. Each atom consists of three main particles:

  • Protons ‚Äî positively charged particles found in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons ‚Äî neutral particles that stabilize the nucleus.
  • Electrons ‚Äî negatively charged particles orbiting the nucleus in energy levels.

The number of protons determines the atomic number and identifies the element. For example, hydrogen has one proton, helium has two, and oxygen has eight. The arrangement of electrons in their shells defines an atom‚Äôs chemical behavior. Atoms often gain, lose, or share electrons to form stable compounds.

Tip: Electrons want to have a full outer shell. Atoms react to reach a more stable electron arrangement.

üî• Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction occurs when substances, called reactants, change to form new substances known as products. This happens because chemical bonds are broken and new ones are formed.

Common signs of a chemical reaction include:

  • Change in color
  • Formation of gas or bubbles
  • Temperature change (heat released or absorbed)
  • Formation of a solid (precipitate)
  • Light or sound emission

Example: burning magnesium in oxygen produces a bright flame and magnesium oxide ‚Äî a new compound. Reactions may release energy (exothermic) or absorb energy (endothermic).

Note: Balancing chemical equations ensures mass is conserved ‚Äî the number of each atom type is the same on both sides.

üíß States of Matter

Matter exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas.

  • Solids ‚Äî fixed shape and volume; particles are tightly packed and vibrate in place.
  • Liquids ‚Äî fixed volume but take the shape of their container; particles move past each other.
  • Gases ‚Äî neither fixed shape nor volume; particles move quickly and spread out.

Transitions between these states ‚Äî melting, freezing, condensation, evaporation, and sublimation ‚Äî involve energy changes. These are physical changes because the chemical identity of the substance stays the same.

Example: Ice melting into water is a physical change; water remains H‚ÇÇO throughout.

üß© The Periodic Table

The Periodic Table arranges elements by increasing atomic number. Elements in the same vertical column (group) often share chemical properties.

  • Metals ‚Äî usually shiny, malleable, and good conductors (left side).
  • Non-metals ‚Äî dull, brittle, and poor conductors (right side).
  • Noble gases ‚Äî unreactive gases in the far right column.

The table helps predict reactivity. For instance, alkali metals (group 1) are very reactive with water, while noble gases are stable.

üî¨ Mixtures and Compounds

A mixture contains two or more substances physically combined (e.g., air, trail mix). Each substance retains its own properties and can be separated physically.

A compound is formed when elements chemically combine in fixed ratios (e.g., water H‚ÇÇO). Compounds have different properties from their component elements and require chemical reactions to separate.

‚ö†Ô∏è Lab Safety Rules

Safety is the most important part of laboratory work. Follow these rules:

  • Wear safety goggles, gloves, and a lab coat.
  • Never eat or drink in the lab.
  • Read labels and instructions before using chemicals.
  • Know the locations of safety equipment (eyewash, extinguisher, first aid).
  • Report spills and accidents immediately.

Good scientists are careful scientists ‚Äî always think ahead and handle chemicals responsibly.
